Foundations of Mathematics
An introduction to what mathematics truly is — its language, its logic, and its beauty.

What is Mathematics?

Mathematics is the science of pattern, structure, and logical reasoning. It is both a language — precise and universal — and a tool for describing everything from the orbit of planets to the encryption of messages. At its core, mathematics is the study of abstract objects and the relationships between them.

"Mathematics is not about numbers, equations, computations, or algorithms — it is about understanding." — William Paul Thurston

Unlike other sciences, mathematical truth does not depend on experiment or observation. A theorem proven today will remain true forever. This permanence makes mathematics both uniquely powerful and uniquely beautiful.

The Language of Mathematics

Every discipline has a language, and mathematics is no exception. The mathematical language is built from numbers, variables, functions, and logical connectives.

Number Systems

ℕ — Natural Numbers
1, 2, 3, 4, … — used for counting
ℤ — Integers
…−2, −1, 0, 1, 2, … — includes negatives
ℚ — Rationals
p/q form — fractions and terminating decimals
ℝ — Real Numbers
All points on the number line, including irrationals like √2 and π
ℂ — Complex Numbers
a + bi, where i = √(−1) — extends the reals into two dimensions
𝕡 — Primes
Integers > 1 with no divisors other than 1 and themselves

Variables & Functions

A variable is a symbol standing for an unknown or varying quantity. A function f maps each input to exactly one output — written f(x) = x² + 1, it encodes an infinite table in a single compact rule.

Mathematical Proof

A proof is a logical argument establishing truth beyond any doubt. Unlike scientific evidence, a mathematical proof is absolute. Common techniques include:

  • Direct proof — assume the hypothesis and deduce the conclusion step by step.
  • Proof by contradiction — assume the opposite is true, derive a contradiction.
  • Mathematical induction — prove a base case, then prove n → n+1.
  • Proof by contrapositive — prove "not B implies not A" to establish "A implies B".
  • Constructive proof — demonstrate existence by explicitly constructing the object.
"The essence of mathematics lies in its freedom." — Georg Cantor

Why Study Mathematics?

Mathematics trains the mind to reason clearly, spot patterns, and solve problems systematically. It underpins every modern technology — from the algorithms powering search engines to encryption protecting your data, from medical imaging to financial modeling.

What is Discrete Mathematics?

Discrete mathematics deals with objects that can be counted individually — integers, graphs, logical statements, and sequences. Unlike calculus, which studies continuous change, discrete math studies structures that are fundamentally separate and countable. It is the mathematical language of computer science, cryptography, and combinatorics.

"Discrete mathematics is the poetry of logic." — Anonymous

Every algorithm you run, every network packet that travels, every database query executed — all rest on discrete mathematical foundations.

Logic & Propositions

A proposition is a statement that is either true (T) or false (F). Logical connectives combine propositions:

∧ AND (Conjunction)
p ∧ q is true only when both p and q are true.
∨ OR (Disjunction)
p ∨ q is true when at least one of p or q is true.
¬ NOT (Negation)
¬p flips the truth value of p.
→ Implication
p → q is false only when p is true and q is false.
↔ Biconditional (IFF)
p ↔ q is true when p and q have the same truth value.
⊕ XOR
p ⊕ q is true when exactly one of p or q is true.

Key Laws

De Morgan's Laws: ¬(p ∧ q) ≡ ¬p ∨ ¬q, and ¬(p ∨ q) ≡ ¬p ∧ ¬q. A tautology is always true (e.g., p ∨ ¬p). A contradiction is always false (e.g., p ∧ ¬p).

Set Theory

A set is a well-defined collection of distinct objects. Sets are the fundamental building blocks of all modern mathematics.

  • Membership: x ∈ A means x is an element of set A.
  • Subset: A ⊆ B means every element of A is also in B.
  • Union A ∪ B: all elements in A or B (or both).
  • Intersection A ∩ B: only elements in both A and B.
  • Difference A − B: elements in A but not in B.
  • Power Set 𝒫(A): all subsets of A — if |A| = n, then |𝒫(A)| = 2ⁿ.
  • Cartesian Product A × B: all ordered pairs (a, b) with a ∈ A, b ∈ B.
The empty set ∅ is a subset of every set — there are no elements that could violate the subset condition.

Relations & Functions

A relation on a set describes how elements are related. An equivalence relation is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive — it partitions a set into equivalence classes.

A function f: A → B assigns to every a ∈ A exactly one f(a) ∈ B. Functions can be injective (one-to-one), surjective (onto), or bijective (both — a perfect pairing).

Combinatorics

Fundamental Counting Principle: if task 1 can be done in m ways and task 2 in n ways, together they can be done in m × n ways.

Permutations vs Combinations

Permutation P(n,k) = n!/(n−k)! — ordered arrangement, order matters (ABC ≠ BAC).
Combination C(n,k) = n!/(k!(n−k)!) — unordered selection, order does not matter.

Pigeonhole Principle

If n+1 objects are placed into n containers, at least one container holds two or more objects. This proves, for example, that in any group of 13 people, at least two share a birth month.

Number Theory Essentials

Divisibility: a | b if b = a × k for some integer k. GCD(a,b) is the largest integer dividing both. LCM(a,b) = ab / GCD(a,b).

Modular Arithmetic: a ≡ b (mod n) means n divides (a − b). This "clock arithmetic" underlies cryptography and hashing.

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic: every integer > 1 has a unique prime factorization.

There are infinitely many prime numbers — Euclid's proof of this, over 2,000 years old, remains one of the most elegant arguments in all of mathematics.

Graph Theory

A graph G = (V, E) consists of vertices V and edges E connecting pairs of vertices. Graphs model networks, relationships, and pathways.

  • Degree: number of edges incident to a vertex.
  • Path: sequence of vertices connected by edges, no repetition.
  • Cycle: a closed path returning to its starting vertex.
  • Tree: a connected graph with no cycles — n vertices, n−1 edges.
  • Eulerian path: visits every edge exactly once.
  • Hamiltonian path: visits every vertex exactly once.
